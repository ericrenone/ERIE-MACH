## Part I · Mach's Principle as the Cosmic Conditional-Independence Boundary

### I.1 A Thought Experiment: The Bucket and the Empty Universe

Newton hung a bucket of water from a twisted cord, let it spin, and watched the surface climb the walls into a paraboloid. The water, he reasoned, was rotating with respect to absolute space, and absolute space exerted the centrifugal effect. The bucket was Newton's evidence for an unobservable backdrop.

Mach refused the backdrop. The water's surface climbs, he said, not because the water rotates relative to nothing, but because it rotates relative to *the earth and the fixed stars and the rest of the matter of the universe*. Take away the stars, take away the earth, take away every other body — leave only the bucket and the water alone in an otherwise empty cosmos — and Mach's claim is sharp and falsifiable in principle: the water would *not* climb the walls. There would be no privileged frame to rotate with respect to. Centrifugal force, in a truly empty universe, would not exist.

Now ask the operational question. To predict whether the water climbs, what must you know? You do not need the position of every atom in every distant galaxy. You need one thing: the averaged distribution of matter within the region that can causally influence the bucket. Given that averaged distribution, the detailed configuration of the rest of the universe — which star is where, which galaxy spins which way at the far edge of the observable cosmos — makes no difference whatsoever. The local inertial behaviour of the bucket is **conditionally independent of the distant detail given the horizon-averaged matter distribution**.

This is the exact statement of a conditional-independence boundary. Writing I for the local inertial behaviour, B for the horizon-averaged matter distribution, and E for the fine-grained cosmic exterior:

P(I | B) = P(I | B, E).

Mach's principle is not a vague philosophical preference. It is the assertion that the causal horizon is a conditional-independence boundary, and that the horizon-averaged matter distribution is a *sufficient statistic* for local inertia.

### I.2 The Schmid Theorem: Mach's Principle Made Exact

The cosmological-gravitomagnetism results of Christoph Schmid converted Mach's principle from a desideratum into a theorem of general relativity itself. The answer, valid for any cosmological equation of state, is yes — and exactly. The time-evolution of a local non-rotating frame follows a specific weighted average of the matter within the Ḣ-radius. Mach's principle — that the axes of local inertial frames precisely track an average of cosmic matter — is a consequence of cosmology with Einstein gravity.

The TH(a,d) reading: the Ḣ-radius **is** the conditional-independence boundary.
